Philosophy 1 | Introduction to Philosophy | SH 1431 | Session B (MTWR 12:30pm-1:35pm) + Discussion
 
 
 

This will be a survey-style course designed to familiarize the student with some basic philosophical issues. We will consider questions such as: Does God exist? If so, then why is there so much suffering in the world? Do we know anything about the external world? What is the relationship between minds and bodies/brains? How do we persist through time even though our physical bodies are constantly changing? Do we have free will? Grades will be determined by two papers and a final exam.

Philosophy 3
|
 Critical Thinking
|
 PHELP 1260 | Session A (MTWR 11:00am-12:05pm) + Discussion
This course is an introduction to elementary logic and critical thinking. We are often engaged in argumentative activities in everyday life, making and evaluating arguments. Unfortunately, our arguments are not always good and sometimes (if not often) we make fallacious reasoning. In this course, we will be concerned with the structure of argumentation and some general methods for distinguishing good reasoning from bad reasoning, aided by some logical techniques. After developing some basic logical concepts, we will look into different systems for representing arguments, including categorical logic and statement logic (a.k.a. propositional logic). We will also go over informal fallacies people frequently make and (if time permits) typical inductive inferences. The course requirements are weekly homework, class participation, and three tests (two midterms and the final).

Philosophy 4
|
 Introduction to Ethics
|
 BUCHN 1930 | Session B (MTWR 11:00am-12:05pm) + Discussion

This will be an introduction to theories and dilemmas in the moral realm. We will survey central strains of thought in the history of moral thinking, as well as study the application of these to real world problems. What has inherent moral value? Is it humanity, reason, or consciousness, or pleasure? Perhaps something completely outside of us, God, or the propagation of the species? Where, when and under what circumstances is killing in the form of abortion, euthanasia, suicide, war, self-defense allowed?

Philosophy 100E
|
 Metaphysics
|
 BREN 1414 | Session B (TWR 9:30am-10:50am)
This course is an introduction to several core topics in contemporary analytic metaphysics (roughly, the study of the fundamental nature of reality). The topics we'll likely cover (and the main questions associated with them) are:
  • Universals:  There are lots of particular things that are, say, male.  But is there something over and above all these instances, like the property (or "universal") of being male?
  • Particulars:  What is the relationship between a particular thing (e.g. me) and its features (e.g. brown hair)?  Is, for example, a particular just a bundle of features? 
  • Modality:  Hilary Clinton might have been elected president in 2008.  What is the nature of this fact?  Is it, say, because there is another possible world in which she was so elected?
  • Time:  What is time exactly?  Is it an objective sort of thing? Can notions like the present be accurately characterized in an objective sort of way?
  • Persistence:  How do things persist through time?  Does, say, the whole thing persist through time or is it merely a series of “time-slices” of that thing?  How especially do people persist through time?  Am I the same person as I was when I was, say, five years old?  If so, why?
The material covered in the course will include selections from important contemporary philosophers (primarily from the last 100 years).
