Philosophy 139/239G
|
 Meta-Ethics
|
 NH 1111 | (MWF 10:00am-10:50am)

The topic of this course is practical reasoning and its relation to ethics. Practical reasoning is reasoning about what to do. Like other forms of reasoning, practical reasoning is subject to standards: one reasons well when one reasons in accord with these standards; one reasons badly when one does not. Our primary aim will be to examine and assess the contemporary state of play in the theory of practical reasoning. Our focus will be on the plausibility of what has become the default position in this area-so-called "instrumentalism"-according to which (very roughly) correct practical reasoning is no more than reasoning well about how to satisfy one's desires. We will be especially interested in exploring alternatives to instrumentalism that treat moral principles as standards of reasoning.


Required Text(s)
Reader only.

Philosophy 150A/250A
|
 Adv. Topics in Ethical Theory
|
 GIRV 2123| (MWF 1:00pm-1:50pm)
Immanuel Kant is one of the most important and influential moral philosophers of all time. Concepts such as respect, dignity, and autonomy-so central to modern moral sensibilities-received their first systematic exposition in his work. The aim of this course is to explore Kant's main contributions to moral philosophy through an examination of his major ethical writings. We will pay special attention to Kant's conception of the aims and methods of moral philosophy and how this conception bears on his substantive ethical views.

Philosophy 150D/252D
|
 Adv. Topics in Phil. of Mind
|
 GIRV 2108 | (TR 3:30pm-4:45pm)
The topic for this course is perception. We will begin by examining classical sense-datum theorists of perception, along with some of their foremost critics. We will study the causal theory of perception in some detail, as well as the theory of vision based in modern cognitive science. We will discuss work on the form of the intentional content of perceptual experience by such philosophers as Burge, Peacocke, and McDowell. We will conclude with some recent work on the interconnnectedness of perception and action.

Reader only.

Philosophy 152/252G
|
 Plato
|
 GIRV 2123 | (MWF 9:00am-9:50am)

Plato's early dialogues and the Republic. Topics will include methodological issues such as Socratic definition and the Socratic method; the moral views of the Platonic Socrates, including Socratic paradoxes, Socratic intellectualism, and Socrates' objections to traditional rhetoric and sophistry; and Plato's metaphysics and epistemology as developed in the middle books of the Republic . A principal goal of the course will be to locate the epistemological and ethical issues raised by these dialogues in the context of contemporary philosophical discussion.

Philosophy 153/253G
|
 Aristotle
|
 GIRV 2116 | (MWF 11:00am-11:50am)

This course is divided into two parts. In the first part, it provides a general introduction to the philosophy of Aristotle focusing on topics such as Aristotle's analysis of change, the structure of the sciences and the role of explanation in natural science, and features of Aristotle's metaphysics and epistemology. In the second part, we study Aristotle's practical philosophy, and in particular his ethics.
