Philosophy 1
|
 Introduction to Philosophy
|
 BUCHN 1910 (MWF 11:00 - 11:50am) + Discussion
This course will give you an introduction to problems and arguments in modern philosophy of mind, theory of knowledge and metaphysics. In philosophy of mind, we will discuss questions like: What is the nature of the mind? Can the mind exist independently of the body? Or is our consciousness simply a physical thing, just like the body? Can any kind of physical thing then be said to have consciuosness, as long as it works like our brain does? In epistemology (theory of knowledge) we will discuss whether we can have knowledge of material things, or whether skepticism about the external world is impossible to refute. When do we accept that we know something? Do we know anything at all? Metaphysics covers discussions about what exists or does not exist. Does God exist? What are the fallacies in arguments for God's existence? Do we have free will? How can we have free will if we accept that the laws of nature are true and therefore determines our behavior as physical beings? How can we have moral responsibility if we are physically determined to behave like we do? This course will teach you how to approach a problem philosophically, that is, to structure a debate in terms of arguments and to critically assess those arguments. We will work with both a text book and with original literature.

Required Text(s)
Appiah, Thinking It Through: An Introduction to Contemporary Philosophy
Instructor:
Hagland
|
 Teaching Assistant(s): Curtin, Evans

Philosophy 3
|
 Critical Thinking
|
 BRDA 1610 (MWF 10:00 - 10:50am) + Discussion
Phil 3 is an introductory course in logic and critical thinking. Specifically, logic is the discipline concerned with correct reasoning and the quality of arguments (whether they are good or bad). The ability to reason is an integral part of any college education as it prepares you to assess, and not be fooled by, the many arguments you will be confronted with in your everyday life (e.g., by politicians, the media, your peers). Phil 3 will also help you in taking standardized tests (like the LSAT and GRE) that test your analytic skills. Anyone interested in learning how to reason well is highly encouraged to take this class.

Required Text(s)
Layman, The Power of Logic
Instructor:
Barnes

Philosophy 100F
|
 Introduction to Philosophy of Science
|
 BLD #387 (TR 11:00am - 12:15pm) + Discussion
Science enjoys enormous prestige in contemporary society. Television commercials frequently declare that some fact has been "scientifically proved." But do scientists ever conclusively prove anything? Aren't they just as biased and fallible as everyone else? Do scientists discover objective features of reality, or do they merely impose their interpretations upon the data? Do electrons really exist, or are they mere theoretical constructs invented by scientists? In short, is science objectively true? We will investigate how various philosophers have approached these questions during the past century. This course counts towards the 36 UD units required for the upper-division major.

Required Text(s)
Godfrey-Smith, Theory and Reality
Kuhn, The Structure of Scientific Revolutions
Reader
Instructor:
Rescorla

Philosophy 116/216G
|
 Meaning and Reference
|
 SH 5617 (MW 11:00am - 12:15pm)
An examination of the classical theories of meaning and reference: John Stuart Mill, Gottlob Frege, Bertrand Russell, Saul Kripke, and the theory of direct reference. Issues to be discussed center on the following: what is the nature of the information content of a declarative sentence, and what do the constituents of the sentence contribute? How is the referent of a term secured? How is the information content of a sentence secured, and how is the contribution of the sentence constituents secured? Solutions to the classical puzzles of meaning and reference are investigated.

Required Text(s)
Garfield, Meaning and Truth
Salmon, Frege's Puzzle
Optional Text(s)
Martinich, Philosophy of Language
Kripke, Naming and Necessity
Seech, Writing Philosophy Papers
Instructor:
Salmon

Philosophy 164/264G
|
 Berkeley
|
 NH 1109 (TR 12:30 - 1:45pm)
This course will examine the philosophy of George Berkeley (1685-1753). We will focus on his immaterialist metaphysics, including his rejection of a mind-independent material reality, his alternative account of the spatial realm as a logical construct from perceptions, and his account of the nature of the thinking self. We will also be looking at his views on meaning and language, and at how these play out in the context of his philosophical theology.

We will focus primarily on Berkeley's major philosophical works, A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge (1710), Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous (1713), and Alciphron (1732), aiming first to understand his theories, second to appreciate their strengths and track down their weaknesses, and third to examine how some of his ideas played out (and continue to play out) in later philosophical epochs. Note: It is recommended (though not required) that students take Phil 160 Descartes or (better yet) Phil 163 Locke before approaching Berkeley.

Required Text(s)
Berkeley, A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge
Berkeley, Three Dialogues Between Hylas and Philonous
Berkeley, Alciphron in Focus
Instructor:
Holden

Philosophy 183/283G
|
 Beginning Modern/Symbolic Logic
|
 SH 1431 (MWF 10:00 - 10:50am) + Discussion
This course is an introduction to the study of correct reasoning--drawing inferences, distinguishing good arguments from bad ones, seeing one's way from assumptions to their consequences. Reasoning is a cognitive process or activity employed in mathematics and other disciplines, as well as in everyday decision making, problem solving, argumentation, and discussion. We shall study the principles and techniques of correct reasoning by developing an artificial symbolic system of "natural deduction," which closely mirrors, but sharpens and systematizes, the patterns of ordinary reasoning that we engage in every day.

Required Text(s)
Kalish, et. al., Logic: Techniques of Formal Reasoning
Instructor:
Salmon

 Teaching Assistant(s): Manning
