Philosophy 4
|
 Introduction to Ethics
|
 GIRV 1004| (MWF 8:00am-8:50am) + Discussion

This is an introductory course in ethics and moral philosophy.  Moral philosophy may be divided into three subfields: normative ethics, metaethics, and applied ethics.  In this course we will consider various questions in each of those fields.  A large part of the course will be devoted to normative ethics.  We will study three main schools of normative thought: Consequentialism, Kantian Ethics, and Virtue Ethics.  In metaethics we will consider various questions concerning the status of moral claims.  Finally, we will examine several contemporary issues in applied ethics.

Philosophy 20C
|
 History of Philosophy
|
 EMBAR| (MWF 1:00pm-1:50pm) + Discussion

This is the third and final course in the PHIL 20 series of classes surveying the history of philosophy in the West. In this last course, we examine the main developments in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries: the so-called early modern period. (Note that you do not have to have taken 20A or 20B in order to enroll for 20C.) We will examine the work of four major figures in the period: John Locke (1632-1704), George Berkeley (1685-1753), David Hume (1711-1776) and Immanuel Kant (1724-1804). The focus of the course will be the epistemological and metaphysical systems developed by these pivotal thinkers, and in particular their various theories of the relationship between mind and world. Reading will consist largely in representative selections from each of these four philosophers.

Philosophy 165/265G
|
 Hume
|
 GIRV 2119| (MW 3:30pm-4:45pm)

This course will examine the philosophy of David Hume (1711-76), addressing his epistemology, metaphysics, ethics, and philosophy of religion. We will focus primarily on Hume?s later philosophical works, the Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, the Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals and the Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion. We will aim first to understand his theories, second to track down their weaknesses, and third to examine how some of his ideas played out (and continue to play out) in later philosophical epochs.

Philosophy 187/287G
|
 Philosophy of Mathematics
|
 GIRV 2123| (MWF 11:00am-11:50am)

We will study philosophical views on the ontology of mathematics. Thus our question will be "What is mathematics about?" Three theories purporting to answer this question are: Structuralism, Fictionalism and Platonism. If time permits, we will consider some recent work on Nominalism.

Texts: Stewart Shapiro (ed.), The Oxford Handbook of Philosophy of Mathematics and Logic, Oxford University Press, 2005

Mark Balaguer, Platonism and Anti-Platonism in Mathematics, Oxford University Press, 1998.

Additional Readings: Articles in philosophical journals.

Philosophy 296C
|
 Seminar in the Philosophy of Language
|
 SH 5617| (M 4:00pm-6:50pm)

According to philosophical mythology, the word 'meter' (as in '3Â½ meters long') was introduced as a term for ("defined as") the length at time t0 of a particular stick S, the so-called standard meter. What then is the length at t0 of S in meters? And how do we know this? Wittgenstein infamously asserted that it cannot be said that S is one meter long, and it also cannot be said that it isn't. Kripke disagreed, holding, also infamously, that the statement 'S is exactly one meter long at t0' expresses a contingent fact that is nonetheless knowable a priori, at least by he/she who so introduced/defined the term. To know the length of a particular stick is to know how long the stick is. Can it really be that one can know a priori how long a particular stick is? This is currently planned as a seminar on controversies concerning the standard meter and especially related issues involving the concept of knowing who and related concepts like those of knowing how long, knowing how many, etc. Among other things, we will go carefully through a forthcoming article of Kripke's on these topics.

Philosophy 296D
|
 Seminar in the Philosophy of Mind
|
 SH 5617| (R 3:00pm-5:50pm)

Bayesian decision theory is the standard theoretical framework for studying reasoning and decision-making under uncertainty. It plays a large role in epistemology, statistics, Artificial Intelligence (including robotics), and cognitive science. We will discuss this framework and its philosophical implications. We will first cover the basic elements of Bayesian decision theory. We will then discuss how the framework bears upon some traditional issues in epistemology and philosophy of science. Finally, we will consider how it relates to cognitive science and philosophy of mind. Throughout the course, we will be especially concerned to evaluate the suggestion that mental activity instantiates Bayesian processes. No prior knowledge of Bayesian decision theory is presupposed, and no mathematical knowledge beyond basic high schoolalgebra is necessary. I will introduce all concepts from scratch, explaining them in an accessible, non-technical way. Readings by Carnap, Chalmers, Davidson, Earman, Jeffrey, Joyce, Lewis, Ramsey, van Fraassen, and others.
