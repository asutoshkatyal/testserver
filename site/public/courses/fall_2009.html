Philosophy 1
|
 Introduction to Philosophy
|
 BUCH 1910| (MWF 11:00am-11:50am) + Discussion

An introduction to the problems and methods of contemporary philosophy, focusing upon metaphysics and epistemology. We will discuss such questions as: Is there life after death? Does God exist? Do I have a soul distinct from my body? What is the nature of consciousness? Could other forms of intelligent life exist, or is cognition the unique endowment of human beings? Could the external world be radically different than I perceive it to be? Readings drawn from both historical and contemporary sources. We will also discuss philosophically relevant items from popular culture, such as the movies The Matrix, The Truman Show, Blade Runner, and others.

Perry, A Dialogue on Personal Identity and Immortality (Hackett, 1978) ISBN-13: 9780915144532
Hofstadter and Dennett, The Mind's I (Basic Books, 2001) ISBN-13: 9780465030910

Philosophy 134/234G
|
 Moral Psychology
|
 GRIV 2123 | (TR 2:00pm-3:15pm)

Moral psychology is an attempt to understand virtue and evil, praise and blame, pride and humility, freedom and responsibility, practical deliberation and action. To explain these phenomena the moral psychologist must answer a number of historically persistent, thorny questions about the nature of action and evaluation. In this course we will concentrate on three different characters--the altruist, the amoralist, and the addict--and the light they throw on some of these questions. Is altruism possible? If it is not, why can't we act for the sake of others? If it is possible, does this mean we can act on something other than our desires? Are there amoralists? Can someone have an entirely correct set of moral views (judgments or opinions) while having no motivation to act morally? If so, does this show that moral judgments are not intrinsically motivating? What is addiction, and how does it relate to more ordinary forms of temptation and weakness of will? What is it to resist a desire or give into temptation? Is it ever irrational to yield to one's strongest desire? If acting on one's strongest desire can be irrational, when is it, and why? When are people responsible for some moral or immoral action? When is it appropriate to praise or blame someone for what she has done? If its initial state and the laws of physics fully determined the future state of the universe, could we freely decide how to act? If free decisions and actions are compatible with the truth of determinism, when, if ever, is a decision or action not free? Requirements for the course consist in two medium length papers and a final exam.

Philosophy 152/252G
|
 Plato
|
 BSIF 1217| (MWF 11:00am-11:50am)
This course is devoted to a careful examination and evaluation of the main philosophical positions advocated and defended by Socrates and Plato, as they appear in Plato's early and middle dialogues. Some of the topics to be covered include: Socratic definition and the Socratic method; the moral views of the Platonic Socrates; Socratic intellectualism; Socrates' objections to traditional rhetoric and sophistry; the paradox of inquiry; the doctrine of recollection; Plato's arguments for the immortality of the soul; Plato's metaphysics and epistemology as developed in the middle books of the Republic and elsewhere; and (if there is time) the emendations to those theories based upon the considerations raised in the Parmenides and Theaetetus. A principal goal of the course will be to locate the epistemological and ethical issues raised by these dialogues in the context of contemporary philosophical discussion.

Philosophy 162/262G
|
 Leibniz
|
 NH 1111 | (MWF 1:00pm-1:50pm)
We will study the metaphysical system of the great rationalist philosopher Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646-1716). Leibniz is said by many to be the greatest systematic metaphysician who ever lived. Nevertheless we shall find that his ideas are very strange from our present viewpoint. Although this is a course in the history of philosophy, we shall be most interested in those doctrines of Leibniz that have some chance of being of value for current philosophical research.
Grades will be based on attendance = 1/6, a midterm examination = 2/6, and a final examination = 3/6.

Philosophy 172/272G
|
 Heidegger
|
 HSSB 3210 | (TR 9:30am-10:45am)

In this course we will closely examine Martin Heideggers first great work, Being and Timeone of the most important and influential philosophical texts of the twentieth century. A good grasp of Heideggers work is essential for understanding many of the major developments in twentieth-century philosophy, including phenomenology, existentialism, hermeneutics, and deconstruction. Our job will be to get clear on Heideggers understanding of topics such as time, subjectivity, history, death, community, and authenticity. Heideggers approach to these issues is notoriously difficult, in part because of the novelty of his language. To address this we will explore the motivations for his decision to depart from more traditional philosophical terminology. This will go hand in hand with an examination of his conception of philosophical method. Why does he take the question of the meaning of being to be so central? How does his commitment to this point of departure set the task for Being and Time? The bulk of the course will focus directly on the reading of Heideggers text. The last section of the course will be devoted to examining some of the more interesting and important responses to his work.

Required Text(s)

Heidegger; Stambaugh, translator; Being and Time (SUNY Press, 1996) ISNB-13: 9780791426784

Philosophy 296E
|
 Seminar in Metaphysics
|
 SH 5617 | (W 3:30pm-6:20pm)

We will discuss perception, with an emphasis on vision. We will study both the philosophy and the science of perception. Vision science is one of the best developed areas of contemporary psychology, and it raises a number of important philosophical issues. For instance, we will discuss connections with the computational theory of mind and with Bayesian psychological models. We will also discuss recent philosophical literature, such as debates over disjunctivism and non-conceptual content.
